1. Introduction to GS1

1.1 What GS1 is and why it exists

GS1 is a global, not-for-profit standards organisation that provides a common system for uniquely identifying products, locations, assets, and parties across supply chains. Its standards (identifiers and barcodes) are used by over two million organisations in more than 120 countries to support efficient, interoperable data exchange. [1][2]

In healthcare, GS1 standards are crucial for accurate product identification, automated data capture, and traceability. These elements are vital for ensuring patient safety, regulatory compliance, and operational efficiency. NHS Supply Chain has designated GS1 as the preferred data coding standard for product identification and has issued policy requirements for suppliers of medical devices and clinical consumables to adopt GS1 identifiers and barcodes under Scan4Safety and related procurement policies. [18][2]

1.2 Core GS1 Identifiers

Key GS1 identification keys are set out in the NHS standard for automatic identification and data capture, DAPB0108. This includes the following keys: [2][3][4][60]

Global Trade Item Number (GTIN) – uniquely identifies a trade item (product or service) at any packaging level (unit, case, pallet), with length options of 8, 12, 13, or 14 digits. GTINs are globally unique when correctly allocated and are used as the device identifier (UDI-DI) in regulatory UDI systems. [4][12][18][2][58][59]   [CS1] 

Global Location Number (GLN) – uniquely identifies physical locations (sites, wards, rooms, bed spaces, shelves), legal entities (trusts, suppliers) and functions of the organisations (departments, sub-divisions). GLNs support precise recording of where assets, equipment are located, commissioned, and used, and where patients are located. The use of this data improves productivity through locating vital equipment quickly, increasing efficiency for facilities maintenance and improving patient flow. [9][2]   [CS2] 

Global Individual Asset Identifier (GIAI) – uniquely identifies an individual asset, such as a specific MRI scanner or infusion pump, rather than the product model. GIAIs support lifecycle tracking of capital equipment, including commissioning, maintenance, relocation, and disposal. [20][21][2][4] They are adopted for asset management and tracking solutions so that a specific asset can be located to improve visibility and improve efficiency in facilities management. They are also used for sample management and pathology systems ensuring that they can be traced across the different departments and systems.     [CS3] 

Global Service Relation Number (GSRN) – identifies a person in their role relative to an organisation (for example, healthcare professional or contractor) and is included in the NHS standard for positive patient identification. [61]

Identifiers are constructed from a GS1 Company Prefix allocated by GS1 member organisations (e.g. GS1 UK), with clear allocation rules to avoid duplication and to trigger new identifiers when products change significantly. [18][4]

1.3 Data Carriers: Barcodes, DataMatrix, and Digital Link

GS1 identifiers are encoded in machine-readable data carriers to support fast, accurate scanning: [5]

GS1 DataMatrix (2D) – a compact two-dimensional symbol capable of encoding GTIN plus production information (batch/lot, serial number, expiry date) in a small footprint. It is the  mandated data carrier of DCB1077 for patient identification and the preferred carrier for medical devices and widely used to meet UDI requirements. [5][6]

GS1-128 (linear) – a one-dimensional barcode that can encode GTIN and additional data using GS1 Application Identifiers; it is larger and typically used where 2D scanning is not yet available. [4]

GS1 Digital Link – expresses GS1 identifiers as web URIs that can be embedded in 2D symbols such as QR codes or DataMatrix, enabling devices to be linked to context-specific digital resources (IFUs, safety alerts, maintenance records) when scanned. [8]

GS1 standards were mandated by the eprocurement strategy in 2014. This led to the DHSC GS1 and PEPPOL standards adoption programme which was renamed Scan4Safety in 2016. Scan4Safety is a priority of the NHS Strategic Commercial Framework and the progamme is led by the NHS England patient safety directorate.

GS1 standards are in the NHS Terms and Conditions for the provision of goods and services, are embedded in the National Equipment Tracking Information System (NETIS) and the Product Information Master.

 

NHS Supply Chain’s Supplier Requirements specify that suppliers must include UDI-compliant GS1 barcodes on all medical devices and GTIN-compliant barcodes on clinical consumables at all packaging levels, with GS1 DataMatrix strongly recommended as scanning infrastructure matures. [18[GL4] ]

1.4 Why GS1 matters for Healthcare and NHP[GL5] 

Adoption of GS1 in healthcare brings four main benefits: [2][1]

Patient safety: Reduces manual transcription errors, supports positive patient identification, and enables precise recall of affected devices.

Traceability: Provides end-to-end visibility of devices and consumables, from manufacturer to point of care and across the equipment lifecycle. [38]

Interoperability: Offers system-agnostic identifiers that can be used consistently across heterogeneous local and national systems (EHRs, CMMS, procurement, NETIS). [2]

Efficiency: Underpins Scan4Safety-type gains in inventory reduction, clinical time released, and improved financial control. [10]

For the New Hospital Programme, GS1 provides a common, national “data language” for equipment and consumables, enabling consistent identification, traceability, and interoperability across all new hospitals, and supporting alignment with NETIS and the wider NHS equipment estate from design through to operation and asset lifecycle management. For the New Hospital Programme, GS1 provides a common language for equipment data across all new hospitals and, through integration with NETIS, across the wider NHS equipment estate. [39][40][41]

2. GS1 in Healthcare Practice

This section describes how GS1 standards are already used in real-world healthcare settings, both within the NHS and internationally. It focuses on practical, operational workflows rather than theoretical models, illustrating how standardised identification and scanning underpin safer care, more efficient operations, and improved data quality. These examples provide an evidence-based foundation for applying GS1 at scale within the New Hospital Programme.

2.1 Clinical and Operational use

GS1 standards are already embedded in a wide range of clinical and operational workflows within the NHS and internationally, providing practical evidence of their effectiveness at scale. [2]

In clinical settings, point-of-care scanning of patient wristbands, medical devices, consumables, and locations enables accurate capture of person–product–place information during care delivery. By using GS1 identifiers encoded in barcodes, these workflows support positive patient identification, reduce reliance on manual data entry, and improve the accuracy of clinical documentation. This approach underpins the “five rights” of medication and device use and enables data captured once to be reused across multiple systems. [9][42]

Operationally, GS1 identifiers support end-to-end supply chain and logistics processes. GTINs and related logistics identifiers enable accurate ordering, receipt, storage, and movement of stock, improving availability while reducing waste and administrative effort. Within asset management functions, GIAIs are used by CMMS and EAM systems to track individual assets across commissioning, maintenance, repair, relocation, and disposal, providing a consistent view of asset status and history. [1][21][22][20]

Nationally, NHS Supply Chain’s Supplier Product Data Standards policy reinforces these practices by requiring GTIN allocation at all packaging levels, inclusion of standard identifiers in catalogue submissions, and use of UDI-compliant GS1 barcodes on medical devices and clinical consumables. Together, these established clinical and operational uses demonstrate that GS1 standards are mature, proven, and directly applicable to the needs of the New Hospital Programme. [19][18]

2.2 Scan4Safety: Person–Product–Place

Scan4Safety, launched by NHS England and DHSC, demonstrates the benefits of GS1-enabled scanning workflows at scale. The approach is based on scanning three elements: [11]

Person: Patient wristband encodes identity (NHS number and/or GSRN), supporting positive patient identification.

Product: Medical devices, consumables, medicines, medical equipment assets carry GS1 identifiers encoded in a data carrier such as a barcode or RFID tag enabling precise recording of what was used. [9]

Place: Locations (Trusts, wards, bed spaces) are labelled with GLNs, allowing events to be linked to exact sites in the patient pathway. [9]

Data captured once at the point of care is reused across EHRs, inventory management systems, finance and quality reporting, embodying the “scan once, use many times” principle. [9]

2.3 Patient safety and recall benefits

The patient safety benefits observed in Scan4Safety demonstrator sites arise directly from the consistent use of GS1 identifiers and scan-based capture of person–product–place data at the point of care. By replacing manual transcription with automated identification, trusts are able to establish reliable links between patients, devices, consumables, and locations, creating a level of traceability that is difficult to achieve through traditional paper-based or manually keyed systems. [10]

This capability has had a particularly significant impact on recall management. At Leeds Teaching Hospitals, the introduction of GS1 barcodes and traceability systems reduced the time required to identify and locate products subject to recall from an average of 8.33 days to approximately 35 minutes. This improvement not only reduced operational cost, saving an estimated £84,000 per year, but also substantially lowered the risk of patients being exposed to affected devices while investigations were ongoing. [10]

Improvements have also been observed in medication safety and clinical workflow reliability. Royal Cornwall Hospitals reported a 76% reduction in preventable medication errors within pharmacy processes following the implementation of barcode-driven scanning, demonstrating how standardised identification can directly reduce human error in high-risk environments. [10] Across six Scan4Safety demonstrator sites, the reduction in manual data entry and record searching released approximately 140,000 hours of clinical time, allowing staff to focus more directly on patient care. [10]

Taken together, these outcomes demonstrate that the safety and efficiency gains attributed to Scan4Safety are not programme-specific effects, but the result of systematic application of GS1 standards for identification and data capture. Unique identifiers, standardised barcodes, and scan-based workflows are therefore directly transferable to national-scale equipment management within the New Hospital Programme.

2.4 International adoption

International regulatory and healthcare system adoption of GS1 standards demonstrates a clear global convergence around common identifiers and data carriers for medical devices and pharmaceuticals. Rather than reflecting isolated national choices, these developments indicate an emerging baseline for how products are identified, traced, and regulated across healthcare systems. [2][5][13]

In the United States, GS1 is an FDA-accredited issuing agency for Unique Device Identification (UDI), with GTIN and GS1 DataMatrix widely used to meet UDI requirements for medical devices and to support pharmaceutical traceability under the Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA). This has driven large-scale adoption of GS1 identifiers by manufacturers, distributors, and healthcare providers, embedding standardised identification into routine clinical and supply chain workflows. [13][15]

Similarly, within the European Union, the Medical Devices Regulation (MDR), In Vitro Diagnostic Regulation (IVDR), and the Falsified Medicines Directive mandate UDI and serialisation requirements that are most commonly implemented using GS1 identifiers and GS1 DataMatrix barcodes. These frameworks reinforce consistent identification at both product and production levels, supporting post-market surveillance, recalls, and cross-border interoperability. [14][5]

The significance of this international alignment for the New Hospital Programme is twofold. First, it reduces supplier burden by allowing manufacturers to support UK requirements using the same identifiers and labelling approaches already deployed globally. Second, it future-proofs national equipment data architectures by aligning them with prevailing international standards rather than bespoke national solutions. As a result, adoption of GS1 within NHP positions new hospitals to operate within a globally harmonised ecosystem for medical device identification, traceability, and regulation.

3. Incorporating GS1 into the NHP equipment database

A national NHP equipment database must integrate data from multiple organisations, suppliers, and local systems while maintaining a single, trusted view of equipment assets. This section sets out how GS1 identifiers can be used as the structural backbone of such a database, enabling consistent product and asset identity across Trusts, suppliers, and national systems. The intent is not to replace existing local identifiers, but to harmonise them through globally recognised standards.

3.1 GS1 Identifiers as Canonical Keys

A national NHP equipment database must harmonise data from multiple sources: suppliers, NHS Supply Chain, Trusts, CMMS/EAM, NETIS, into a consistent, trusted asset register. Using GS1 identifiers as canonical keys provides a robust approach: [40][41]

GTIN as canonical product identifier: Each device model and configuration is identified by a unique GTIN, independent of supplier or Trust. [2][4]

GIAI as canonical asset identifier: Each individual asset instance carries a unique GIAI, supporting lifecycle tracking regardless of where it is located. [4][20][2]

Local catalogue codes and asset tags can remain in use, mapped to these canonical identifiers in the national database.

3.2 Supporting consistent product identity across Trusts

Currently, the same equipment model may appear under different names and codes in different Trusts, obstructing national benchmarking and procurement aggregation. A GTIN-centric model addresses this: [24][43]

Suppliers register GTIN-based product master data with NHS Supply Chain, including technical specifications, regulatory attributes, and packaging hierarchies. [19][18]

Trusts map local product codes to GTINs; these mappings are stored centrally, enabling cross-organisation comparison and consolidation. [24]

The NHP database and NETIS use GTINs as primary keys for equipment types, supporting consistent reporting on volumes, utilisation, and cost across the NHS. [41][40]

This builds directly on existing supplier requirements and NHS Supply Chain systems rather than introducing new proprietary codes. [18]

3.3 Supplier provided master data

GS1 practice assigns primary responsibility for product master data to manufacturers and brand owners, who are best placed to maintain accurate information. NHS Supply Chain’s policy implements this for the NHS: [24][18]

Suppliers must be GS1 members and allocate GTINs to all in-scope products at all packaging levels.

GTINs must be included in catalogue templates and other data submissions to NHS Supply Chain.

Suppliers are responsible for keeping product data current and correcting quality issues identified by customers or NHS Supply Chain. [19][18]

The national NHP database can ingest this supplier-maintained data once and redistribute it to Trusts, significantly reducing duplication and inconsistencies seen in local catalogues. [24]

3.4 Asset tracking and lifecycle management

Taken together, GTIN and GIAI enable continuous visibility of equipment across its entire lifecycle, from initial planning through to disposal. [20][21][38]

At the planning and procurement stage, the combination of GTINs (to identify equipment types) and GIAIs (to identify individual asset instances) allows national and local planners to understand what equipment is in service, where it is located, and how it is distributed across the estate. Age profiles, quantities, and standardisation levels can be analysed consistently across Trusts, supporting evidence-based decisions on capital investment, replacement planning, and specification alignment. During commissioning, GIAIs are assigned to individual assets as they are accepted into service, with installation details, acceptance testing results, configuration data, and initial locations recorded against a single, persistent identifier. [32][40][21]

Once operational, the same identifiers underpin day-to-day asset management and performance monitoring. Usage events and utilisation metrics can be linked to GIAI and GTIN to support capacity analysis, cost-per-procedure modelling, and informed redistribution of assets. Maintenance activities, including preventive servicing, calibration, repairs, and safety checks, are recorded against the same GIAI, creating a complete and auditable maintenance history for each asset. This continuity supports regulatory compliance, improves reliability analysis, and enables more predictive approaches to maintenance and replacement. [23][40][22][20]

When assets reach end of life, decommissioning and disposal events are recorded against the GIAI, closing the lifecycle while retaining a complete historic record for audit, safety, and assurance purposes. [21]

3.5 Local codes, asset tags and legacy identifiers

While GS1 identifiers provide the canonical mechanism for identifying products and assets within the NHP equipment database, it is neither practical nor desirable to mandate immediate replacement of all existing local identifiers used by NHS Trusts. Local catalogue codes, asset tags, and legacy identifiers are deeply embedded in operational workflows, historical records, and local systems, and must therefore be accommodated in a controlled manner. [21][24]

Within the NHP data model, such identifiers should be treated explicitly as secondary references rather than as authoritative identifiers. Local catalogue codes and stock-keeping units may continue to be used for local operational purposes, butpurposes but must be mapped one-to-one to GTINs within centrally governed reference tables. This ensures that local variation does not propagate into national reporting, analytics, or assurance processes. [24][18]

Similarly, local asset tags applied by NHS Trusts can be recorded as secondary identifiers linked to GIAIs. This allows existing scanning and maintenance workflows to continue locally, while ensuring that each physical asset is represented nationally by a single, persistent identifier. Where assets pre-date GS1 adoption or lack manufacturer-assigned GTINs, internal identifiers may be used on a temporary basis, with retrospective association to GTINs applied where feasible[CS6] . [20][21]

Over time, the reliance on legacy identifiers should reduce as new equipment is commissioned with GS1-compliant identification by default. The role of the national database is therefore not to preserve local coding schemes indefinitely, but to provide a structured transition path in which legacy identifiers are contained, governed, and progressively displaced by standardised identifiers. This approach supports continuity of operations while maintaining the integrity and long-term value of the national equipment dataset. [24]

3.6 Device classification using the Global Medical Device Nomenclature (GMDN)

While GS1 identifiers provide globally unique identity for products, assets and locations, a national NHP equipment database also requires a consistent method for classifying medical devices by type and intended use. This is provided by the Global Medical Device Nomenclature (GMDN), an internationally recognised system that assigns standardised preferred terms and numeric codes to groups of medical devices with similar clinical purpose and technological characteristics. [55][25]

GMDN supports regulatory oversight, post-market surveillance, vigilance reporting and national-level analytics by enabling devices to be grouped consistently regardless of manufacturer, brand name or local catalogue description. In the UK and internationally, GMDN is referenced within medical device regulatory frameworks and is used by competent authorities to support adverse incident reporting, market surveillance and statistical analysis. [56][57]

Within the NHP equipment database, GMDN should be implemented as a classification attribute, complementary to GS1 identifiers rather than a replacement for them. Each equipment record should therefore include a GTIN to identify the specific device model or configuration, a GIAI (where applicable) to identify individual asset instances, and an associated GMDN code and preferred term to describe the generic device category. This mirrors international UDI practice, where UDI-DI (such as GTIN) and GMDN coexist to support both identification and classification. [26]

This layered approach enables robust national reporting and benchmarking. Devices with different GTINs but the same GMDN can be analysed together to assess fleet size, utilisation, reliability and replacement cycles across Trusts and hospital sites, supporting evidence-based standardisation and capital investment decisions within the New Hospital Programme.

Governance of GMDN data should be managed centrally as part of the national product master, with manufacturers responsible for assigning appropriate GMDN codes and these being validated during supplier onboarding. Trusts should consume GMDN as reference data rather than curate it locally, ensuring consistency across the estate and alignment with NETIS and other national reporting systems. [24]

4. Traceability and Event Data

While unique identifiers establish what an item is, effective equipment management depends on understanding how that item changes over time. This section explains how GS1 traceability standards support the capture of lifecycle events, such as commissioning, movement, maintenance, and disposal, creating a continuous, auditable history of equipment activity. This event-based approach is essential for safety, regulatory compliance, and operational insight at national scale.

4.1 Traceability beyond static identification

Traceability requires more than static identifiers; it depends on capturing event data that answers “what, where, when and why” for each relevant item over time. In an equipment context, this includes: [16][38]

·         Commissioning events (asset created and brought into service).

·         Movement events (asset relocated between wards or sites).

·         Usage events (asset used in a clinical procedure).

·         Maintenance events (asset serviced, repaired or calibrated).

·         Decommissioning and disposal events. [38][20][21]

These event records form a digital history that supports operational management, incident investigation and regulatory compliance.

4.2 GS1 EPCIS for event capture

To support consistent and interoperable capture of lifecycle events, GS1 defines the Electronic Product Code Information Services (EPCIS) standard as a common event data model and exchange mechanism. [17][15]

EPCIS provides a structured way of recording and sharing event data that answers four fundamental questions: what happened, where it happened, when it happened, and why it happened. Events reference GS1 identifiers such as GTINs and GIAIs to identify the object involved, and GLNs to identify the location, alongside standardised timestamps, business steps, and dispositions. This creates a consistent, machine-readable record of asset activity that can be exchanged across systems and organisational boundaries. [17][15]

For NHP equipment, EPCIS can be used to represent key lifecycle events including commissioning of assets into service, movement between locations or sites, maintenance and calibration activities recorded by CMMS or EAM systems, and decommissioning or disposal at end of life. Suppliers may also emit EPCIS events for shipments, deliveries, and returns, enabling traceability from manufacturer through to operational use. [17][15]

By using EPCIS as a shared event model, the NHP equipment database can ingest and harmonise lifecycle data from multiple sources without relying on bespoke integrations. This supports scalable national traceability while allowing Trusts and suppliers to retain their existing operational systems.

4.3 Benefits: recalls, safety, utilisation and audit[GL7] 

When applied at scale, EPCIS-based traceability delivers tangible safety, operational, and assurance benefits for healthcare organisations. [10]

Rapid recall management is one of the most significant benefits. By linking devices to standard identifiers, locations, and event histories, affected equipment can be identified and located within minutes rather than days, as demonstrated by Scan4Safety implementations that achieved substantial reductions in recall response times. This capability reduces patient risk, limits service disruption, and lowers the operational cost of recall investigations. [27][10]

Event-based traceability also strengthens patient safety and post-market surveillance by enabling patterns of adverse incidents, faults, or failures to be associated with specific devices, batches, or models. This supports targeted interventions, informed risk management, and timely regulatory reporting, while avoiding unnecessary removal of unaffected equipment from service. [38]

From an operational perspective, aggregated EPCIS event data supports utilisation analysis and fleet optimisation by providing insight into how often assets are used, where bottlenecks occur, and where redistribution or replacement may be required. At the same time, comprehensive, timestamped event histories create auditable records of commissioning, maintenance, movement, and disposal, supporting inspection readiness for MHRA, CQC, and internal assurance processes. [23][40][20][45][28]

5. Regulatory and Standards alignment

5.1 UDI and GS1

Unique Device Identification (UDI) is a regulatory framework designed to ensure that medical devices can be unambiguously identified, traced, and monitored throughout their lifecycle. Its primary purpose is to strengthen patient safety, post-market surveillance, and recall effectiveness by enabling devices to be consistently identified across manufacturers, healthcare providers, and regulators. [13][14]

Under UDI, each medical device carries a unique identifier composed of two elements: a Device Identifier (UDI-DI), which identifies the manufacturer and model or version of a device, and one or more Production Identifiers (UDI-PI), which capture variable data such as batch or lot number, serial number, and expiry date. Together, these elements enable both product-level identification and instance-level traceability. [13]

GS1 is an accredited issuing agency for UDI and provides a mature, globally adopted mechanism for implementing UDI requirements in practice. In GS1-based implementations, the Global Trade Item Number (GTIN) commonly fulfils the role of the UDI-DI, while GS1 Application Identifiers are used to encode UDI-PI elements such as batch/lot number, expiry date, and serial number within machine-readable barcodes, most commonly GS1 DataMatrix. [12][13][46]

From an NHP perspective, the significance of this alignment is that UDI compliance does not require a separate or bespoke identification scheme. By adopting GS1 identifiers as the foundation of the national equipment database, NHP can meet current UK, EU, and international UDI requirements while also supporting operational workflows such as commissioning, maintenance, and recall management through the same identifiers. This reduces duplication, simplifies supplier onboarding, and ensures consistency between regulatory and operational data. [14][18]

In addition to UDI identification, regulatory frameworks reference the Global Medical Device Nomenclature (GMDN) as a standard mechanism for classifying devices by intended use and technology. Within the NHP equipment database, GMDN should be treated as a complementary classification attribute rather than an alternative identifier. Each device record should therefore combine a GS1-based UDI-DI (such as a GTIN) for precise identification with a GMDN code to support grouping, analysis, and regulatory reporting. This mirrors international best practice and ensures alignment between identification, classification, and surveillance activities. [26][56]

Taken together, UDI and GS1 provide not only regulatory compliance but also a coherent design pattern for the NHP equipment database, enabling a single set of identifiers to support safety, assurance, and operational use across the full equipment lifecycle.

5.2 Regulatory compliance, audit readiness and assurance

Regulatory compliance and assurance in healthcare depend not only on meeting formal requirements, but on the ability to demonstrate, at any point in time, that devices are correctly identified, traceable, maintained, and safe to use. A GS1-based approach to equipment data directly supports this by providing consistent, verifiable identifiers and auditable records across the full equipment lifecycle. [13][14]

By using UDI-compliant GTINs as the primary product identifiers and GIAIs to identify individual asset instances, the NHP equipment database enables regulators and auditors to unambiguously determine what device is in use, where it is located, and how it has been managed over time. This supports core regulatory activities including post-market surveillance, field safety corrective actions, and recall investigations, where rapid identification and localisation of affected devices is critical. [27][28]

Audit readiness is strengthened through the creation of complete, time-stamped lifecycle records linked to individual assets. Maintenance, calibration, inspection, movement, and decommissioning events recorded against a persistent GIAI create a continuous audit trail that can be readily interrogated during inspections by bodies such as the MHRA and CQC. This reduces reliance on fragmented local records and manual evidence collation, lowering the operational burden associated with inspections and investigations. [45][20][28]

At national scale, the use of standardised identifiers and data structures also improves assurance by enabling consistent aggregation and analysis across Trusts. Patterns of device performance, safety incidents, or maintenance non-compliance can be identified more readily, supporting proactive risk management rather than reactive response. Importantly, this approach aligns regulatory, operational, and analytical needs around a single identification framework, reducing duplication and inconsistency between systems. [18][40]

In this way, adoption of GS1 standards within the NHP equipment database supports not only formal regulatory compliance, but a more robust and sustainable assurance model, one that improves inspection readiness, reduces regulatory risk, and strengthens national oversight of the equipment estate.

6. Data governance and master data management

6.1 National-Scale data governance

Effective national-scale data governance is essential to ensure that equipment data is accurate, trusted, secure, and usable across organisational boundaries. [48]

For the NHP equipment database, data governance must establish clear accountability for the creation, maintenance, validation, and use of data while supporting consistent interpretation across Trusts, suppliers, and national systems. This includes defining ownership of different data domains, agreeing common standards and reference data, and ensuring that data quality expectations are applied uniformly. Without this foundation, the benefits of standardised identifiers and traceability cannot be realised at scale. [49][24]

In practice, governance responsibilities should be distributed according to where expertise and control reside. Manufacturers are best placed to own and maintain product master data, NHS Supply Chain acts as a central point for catalogue curation and validation, Trusts remain responsible for asset-level and operational records, and the national body governs shared reference data, integration rules, and reporting standards. Named data stewards and formal escalation routes are required to resolve data issues and drive continuous improvement. [49][18][24]

Quality controls underpin these arrangements and should include validation of GS1 identifiers, enforcement of mandatory data fields, monitoring of completeness and consistency, and regular review of data timeliness. Together, these measures ensure that the NHP equipment database operates as a trusted national asset rather than a passive data repository. [7][7]

6.2 Central vs Local governance

Effective governance of a national equipment database requires a clear and deliberate division of responsibilities between national and local levels. This division is essential to balance consistency and standardisation with operational ownership and accountability within Trusts. Without such clarity, national initiatives risk either duplicating local effort or undermining local control. [49][24]

At national level, governance should focus on data that must be consistent across the NHS to support interoperability, regulation, and strategic oversight. This includes the product master data associated with GS1 identifiers (such as GTIN and UDI-DI), centrally managed reference data (including GLN structures and classification schemes), and regulatory information such as safety notices and recalls. Central governance of these domains ensures that all Trusts operate against a common, authoritative reference, reducing ambiguity and variation. [18][27][28]

At local level, Trusts remain responsible for data that is inherently operational and context-specific. This includes individual asset records linked to GIAIs, local asset tags, commissioning details, maintenance and calibration records, and day-to-day operational usage data. Trusts are best placed to ensure the accuracy and timeliness of this information, as it reflects local workflows, estates configurations, and service delivery. [20][21][23]

The role of the national NHP equipment database is therefore not to replace local systems, but to act as a harmonisation and assurance layer. By linking centrally governed reference data with locally governed operational records through GS1 identifiers, the database enables national visibility and analysis while preserving local accountability and system ownership. [24][40]

6.3 Data quality, versioning and supplier onboarding

High-quality data is a prerequisite for the safe and effective operation of a national equipment database. Within the NHP context, data quality must be designed in from the outset through a combination of supplier requirements, automated validation, and clear rules governing change over time, rather than relying on retrospective correction. [7][18]

Automated[CS8]  data quality controls play a central role in this approach. These include validation of GS1 identifiers such as GTINs, GLNs, and GIAIs; verification of barcode quality against recognised ISO standards; and monitoring of completeness and consistency for mandatory data fields. Exception reporting and remediation workflows allow issues to be identified early and resolved at source, reducing the propagation of errors into downstream systems. [7]

Version management is equally important to maintaining trust in the data. Application of GS1 GTIN Allocation Rules ensures that new identifiers are created when functionally significant changes occur to a device, such as changes affecting form, fit, function, or regulatory status. This prevents different versions of equipment from being conflated under a single identifier and supports accurate lifecycle tracking, recalls, and analytics. [4][18]

Supplier onboarding provides the primary control point at which these quality and versioning requirements are enforced. As part of procurement and catalogue submission processes, suppliers should be required to demonstrate GS1 membership, correct allocation of GTINs across all relevant packaging levels, provision of complete and accurate product master data, and evidence of barcode quality compliance. Embedding these requirements into onboarding and contractual frameworks ensures that data entering the NHP ecosystem is compliant by default. [18][19]

Together, these measures establish a preventative data quality model in which errors are minimised at source, changes are managed transparently, and responsibility is clearly allocated. This approach reduces operational burden on Trusts, supports regulatory assurance, and ensures that the NHP equipment database remains a trusted national asset over time. [24]

6.4 Reducing duplication and ambiguity

A central objective of national data governance is to reduce duplication and ambiguity arising from inconsistent local coding and naming practices. [24]

By using GTIN as the common product identifier and maintaining a single national product master, multiple local catalogue codes and descriptions that refer to the same equipment can be reconciled to a single, authoritative reference. This enables consistent reporting, comparison, and aggregation across Trusts while allowing local systems to retain their own operational identifiers where required. [24][18]

Reducing duplication simplifies system integration, lowers the effort required to onboard new suppliers or Trusts, and reduces the risk of procurement, stocking, or clinical errors caused by misidentification. Over time, this clarity supports more reliable benchmarking, improved procurement leverage, and stronger national oversight of the equipment estate. [24]

Taken together, these governance arrangements ensure that equipment data is consistent, scalable, and trusted at national level without undermining local ownership or operational autonomy.

7. Interoperability, digital architecture, and digital twins

The New Hospital Programme is being delivered within a rapidly evolving digital health landscape characterised by open standards, modular architectures, and increasing use of data-driven decision making. This section explains how GS1 standards align with modern NHS digital principles and how they enable advanced capabilities such as interoperable APIs, trusted digital resources, and digital twins of physical equipment assets.

7.1 GS1 in modern digital health architectures

NHS digital policy emphasises open APIs, FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources), and modular system architectures. GS1 standards integrate naturally into this environment. [29]

FHIR Device resources can carry GTINs and GIAIs as identifiers, enabling clinical and operational systems to reference the same physical assets. [30][31]

EPCIS provides a complementary event model for supply chain and asset lifecycle events, while FHIR focuses on clinical data. [15][17]

GS1 Digital Link makes identifiers web-addressable, enabling mobile and web applications to retrieve equipment information and resources via standard HTTP. [8]

The NHP database should be API friendly to accommodate this as a potential future capability. [29]

7.2 GS1 Digital link for trusted digital resources

While GS1 identifiers provide a stable means of identifying equipment, GS1 Digital Link extends this capability by enabling those identifiers to act as controlled gateways to authoritative digital information. By expressing GS1 identifiers as standard web URIs, Digital Link allows a single physical scan to resolve to trusted, context-appropriate resources without embedding fixed URLs or duplicating links across systems. [8]

In practice, this enables consistent access to equipment-related information across clinical, engineering, and operational roles. Clinicians scanning a device can be directed to the correct instructions for use, safety notices, or local standard operating procedures. Clinical engineering and estates teams can retrieve service manuals, configuration data, maintenance histories, and parts information. Where appropriate, patients may also be directed to approved information about devices used in their care. Crucially, these resources are accessed through the same underlying identifier rather than through locally maintained links or ad hoc document repositories. [8]

For a national programme such as NHP, the primary value of Digital Link lies in governance and control. A centrally managed Digital Link resolver allows national bodies to define which resources are presented for a given identifier, while still permitting local or role-specific variation where required. This reduces the risk of outdated, inconsistent, or unofficial documentation being used in clinical or maintenance contexts and ensures that critical safety information can be updated once and made available everywhere. [8]

From an architectural perspective, Digital Link complements the NHP equipment database by providing a lightweight, standards-based mechanism for connecting physical assets to digital content without tightly coupling systems. It supports modular, API-driven designs and aligns with NHS principles for interoperability and reuse. By incorporating Digital Link as an optional but supported capability, NHP can future-proof equipment information access while maintaining national assurance over content quality and provenance. [8]

7.3 Digital twinning of equipment assets

A digital twin provides a continuously updated virtual representation of a physical asset, reflecting its state, location, and performance over time. [20]

In practical terms, GS1 identifiers provide the stable reference framework required to establish and maintain digital twins of equipment assets. The GTIN anchors product-level master data, including technical specifications and regulatory attributes, while the GIAI uniquely identifies individual asset instances and links them to commissioning, configuration, and maintenance records. GLNs provide spatial context, associating each asset with specific sites, buildings, and rooms. [2][4][20]

As operational and maintenance events are recorded, the digital representation of the asset is updated to reflect real-world usage, condition, and history. This enables predictive maintenance based on utilisation and fault patterns, supports scenario planning for equipment moves or replacements, and allows lifecycle and environmental impacts to be assessed across the estate. [38][22][20][21]

By ensuring that each NHP asset is consistently identified and supported by event data, the programme establishes a scalable foundation for digital twinning that can be expanded incrementally as data maturity increases. [20]

8. Equipment lifecycle and estates integration

8.1 Linking procurement, commissioning, maintenance, moves, and disposal

GS1 identifiers provide continuity across the full operational lifecycle of equipment, linking procurement, estates, clinical use, and disposal into a single, coherent data model. [23][21]

From procurement onwards, GTINs and GLNs enable accurate ordering, delivery reconciliation, and confirmation that equipment is received at the correct organisational and physical locations. Purchase orders, advance shipping notices, and delivery confirmations reference standard identifiers, reducing manual intervention and improving data accuracy. At commissioning, individual assets are assigned GIAIs, with acceptance testing, installation details, and initial locations recorded to establish a trusted baseline record for each item entering service. [51][18][21]

During operation, usage information and aggregated activity metrics can be associated with GIAI and GLN to support utilisation analysis and cost modelling. Maintenance workflows within CMMS or EAM systems reference the same identifiers, ensuring that servicing, calibration, fault resolution, and compliance activities are consistently recorded against the correct asset. Asset relocations are captured by recording changes in GLN, maintaining accurate estates and inventory views across wards, buildings, and sites. [40][23][22][20]

At end of life, decommissioning and disposal events update the asset status associated with the GIAI, preserving a complete lifecycle history to support audit, safety assurance, and environmental reporting. [50][21]

 

8.2 Integration with EAM/CMMS and estates systems

Trusts rely on established Computerised Maintenance Management Systems (CMMS) and Enterprise Asset Management (EAM) platforms to manage day-to-day equipment operations. Any national NHP equipment database must therefore integrate with these systems in a way that preserves local operational control while enabling national visibility and assurance. Attempting to replace or centralise local CMMS/EAM functionality would introduce significant risk and is neither practical nor necessary. [22][20]

Two complementary integration patterns can support this objective. In a national reference, local execution model, the national database provides authoritative product and reference data such as GS1 identifiers, core attributes, and regulatory information which local CMMS/EAM systems consume and apply within their operational workflows. Local systems remain the systems of record for maintenance activities, configuration details, and asset status, while benefiting from consistent national identifiers and reference data. [20][22]

Alternatively, a federated synchronisation model allows local CMMS/EAM systems to remain fully authoritative while sharing selected asset and lifecycle data with the national database through scheduled feeds or APIs. In this pattern, the national platform maintains a consolidated, read-only or lightly curated view of the equipment estate, supporting assurance, analytics, and oversight without interfering in local operations. [40][22]

In both models, GS1 identifiers; GTIN for product identity, GIAI for individual assets, and GLN for location, act as the binding contract between national and local systems. They ensure that data exchanged between CMMS/EAM platforms, procurement systems, estates records, and the NHP database refers to the same physical equipment, regardless of system boundaries or vendor implementations. [20]

By designing integration around these standard identifiers rather than bespoke interfaces, the NHP equipment database can support multiple local systems, evolve over time, and scale nationally without increasing complexity or undermining Trust-level accountability. This approach provides the flexibility needed to accommodate local diversity while maintaining a coherent, assured national view of the equipment estate.

8.3 Capital equipment visibility and fleet management

Effective management of capital equipment at national scale requires a level of visibility and comparability that cannot be achieved through isolated local systems alone. For the New Hospital Programme, this is particularly important given the scale of investment, the long operational life of equipment, and the need to align capital planning across multiple sites and delivery phases. [32][40]

A harmonised, GS1-based equipment database provides a consistent national view of the equipment estate by enabling clear understanding of what equipment exists, where it is located, its age profile, utilisation patterns, and maintenance history across NHP sites and, through integration with NETIS, the wider NHS. This foundational visibility supports informed oversight and reduces reliance on fragmented or manually reconciled data sources. [41][40]

Building on this visibility, standardised identifiers enable meaningful benchmarking across Trusts and equipment models. Performance, reliability, utilisation, and cost data can be compared on a like-for-like basis, allowing variation to be distinguished from genuine clinical or contextual differences. This supports evidence-based identification of best practice, underperforming assets, and opportunities for standardisation or redistribution. [43][32]

At a strategic level, these capabilities enable more robust fleet management and long-term capital planning. Age and condition profiles, combined with utilisation and maintenance data, support the development of coordinated replacement and investment plans over 5–10 year horizons. This reduces reactive procurement, improves value for money, and ensures that capital investment decisions align with service demand, productivity goals, and NHP standardisation objectives. [52][33][39]

Taken together, national fleet visibility and management capability are central to achieving the NHP’s aims of productivity, resilience, and value. By grounding capital decisions in consistent, comparable data, the programme strengthens assurance over public investment while supporting more sustainable management of the NHS equipment estate.

9. Operational, financial and analytical benefits

While GS1-enabled scanning and identification deliver clear operational improvements at local level, the full value of these standards is realised when data is aggregated and analysed at national scale. For the New Hospital Programme, the primary benefit of a GS1-based equipment database is not simply improved local efficiency, but the ability to support consistent, evidence-based decision making across procurement, estates, clinical services, and capital planning. [10][32]

By establishing a common identification framework across organisations and systems, the NHP equipment database enables operational control, financial insight, and analytical capability that cannot be achieved through isolated local datasets alone.

9.1 Inventory and stock management

Inventory represents both a patient safety risk and a significant source of avoidable cost within healthcare systems. Without standardised identification and real-time visibility, Trusts tend to rely on excess stockholding, manual counts, and conservative replenishment strategies to mitigate uncertainty. [34]

GS1-enabled inventory processes replace this uncertainty with reliable, scan-derived data on what stock is held, where it is located, and how it is consumed. Automated identification of products using GTINs enables accurate receipt, movement tracking, and expiry management, reducing waste and improving availability. Evidence from Scan4Safety demonstrator sites shows substantial reductions in expired stock, lower overall inventory levels, and faster, more accurate stock counts compared to manual processes. [10][34]

At national scale, these benefits extend beyond individual Trusts. Aggregated inventory data enables visibility of stock distribution across sites, identification of surplus and shortage patterns, and support for coordinated redistribution or procurement strategies. This reduces duplication, improves resilience, and supports more efficient use of working capital across the NHP and wider NHS estate. [35][10]

9.2 Reducing unwarranted variation

Unwarranted variation in equipment choice, utilisation, and maintenance practice represents a persistent challenge for healthcare systems. Such variation is often driven not by clinical need, but by fragmented data, inconsistent coding, and limited ability to compare like with like across organisations. [36][37]

By using GTINs as canonical product identifiers and harmonising asset and usage data nationally, the NHP equipment database enables meaningful comparison of equipment performance, utilisation, and cost across Trusts. Devices that are nominally similar but coded differently locally can be analysed together, while genuine differences in model, configuration, or usage context can be clearly distinguished. [24][43]

This capability supports identification of outliers, benchmarking against best practice, and evidence-based standardisation where appropriate. Importantly, it allows variation to be interrogated rather than assumed, supporting informed clinical and operational discussion rather than top-down mandates. Over time, this contributes directly to the productivity and efficiency improvements highlighted in national reviews of NHS procurement and operational performance. [37][43]

9.3 Cost-per-Procedure and Value-Based decision making

Linking equipment data to clinical activity enables a shift from asset-focused accounting to outcome-oriented analysis. When GS1-identified equipment usage can be associated with procedures, pathways, or service lines, it becomes possible to calculate equipment-related cost per procedure with far greater accuracy than is currently achievable. [32][36]

At national scale, this capability supports value-based decision making across the New Hospital Programme. Equipment options can be assessed not only on purchase price, but on total cost of ownership, utilisation efficiency, maintenance burden, and contribution to clinical outcomes. This enables more robust evaluation of standardisation proposals, replacement strategies, and investment trade-offs. [32]

For NHP, where capital decisions have long-term implications for service delivery and financial sustainability, this analytical capability is particularly important. By grounding investment decisions in consistent, comparable data, the programme can prioritise resources where they deliver the greatest value, support service redesign, and avoid the downstream costs associated with suboptimal equipment choices. [33][39]

10. Information governance, privacy and security

Effective information governance is essential to the success of a national equipment database, not because such systems require extensive personal data, but because they must demonstrate that patient safety, privacy, and accountability are protected by design. For the New Hospital Programme, the primary information governance objective is therefore risk containment: enabling national visibility and traceability of equipment while avoiding unnecessary collection or centralisation of personal data. [45]

A GS1-based approach supports this objective by allowing equipment, location, and lifecycle data to be managed independently of patient-identifiable information, while still enabling effective recall, assurance, and analytics.

10.1 Managing traceability data without unnecessary personal data

The NHP equipment database should be designed to hold operational and asset-related data rather than patient-identifiable records. Core data elements include GS1 identifiers (GTIN, GIAI, GLN), equipment status, and lifecycle events such as commissioning, maintenance, movement, and disposal. These data support traceability and assurance without requiring direct access to patient identities. [45]

Where clinical workflows link patients to devices (for example, during a procedure), those associations should remain within local clinical systems governed by existing NHS information governance frameworks. The national database need only support identification of affected equipment, batches, or serial numbers during safety investigations or recalls, leaving Trusts to determine patient impact locally. This minimisation approach reduces privacy risk while preserving safety and operational effectiveness.

10.2 Separation of equipment and patient data

Clear separation of equipment data and patient data is central to maintaining trust and compliance. In the NHP operating model, the national database functions as an equipment and asset intelligence platform, not a clinical record system. It provides authoritative information on devices and assets, while patient-identifiable data remains confined to EHRs and local clinical systems. [28]

In practical terms, this means that national-level recall or safety analysis identifies which devices are affected and where they are located, but not which patients received care involving those devices. Trusts retain responsibility for querying their own systems to identify and manage patient follow-up where required. This separation aligns with GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018, and NHS data protection principles, while also supporting scalable national oversight without unnecessary data exposure.

10.3 Access controls, audit trails and retention

Strong access control and audit mechanisms underpin assurance for both national and local stakeholders. Role-based access control (RBAC) should be used to ensure that users can only view or modify data appropriate to their role, such as Trust-specific operational data or nationally aggregated views. This limits the risk of inappropriate access while supporting legitimate operational and analytical use. [47]

Comprehensive audit trails should record access to and changes within the system, providing transparency and accountability for data use. These records support inspection readiness and incident investigation by demonstrating who accessed what data, when, and for what purpose. Retention policies must be clearly defined and aligned with NHS records management guidance and regulatory requirements, ensuring that data is retained for as long as necessary to support safety, assurance, and audit, but no longer. [45][47]

Together, these controls ensure that the NHP equipment database operates within a robust information governance framework that supports national traceability and insight without increasing privacy risk. By embedding minimisation, separation, and accountability into the design, the programme can enable data-driven oversight while maintaining public and regulatory trust.

11. Implementation and adoption risks

Successful adoption of GS1 standards within the NHP is as much an organisational challenge as a technical one. This section outlines the key risks associated with large-scale implementation, including supplier readiness, integration complexity, and change fatigue, and describes practical mitigation strategies based on NHS experience and international best practice.

11.1 Phased implementation for NHP

Large-scale digital and data standardisation programmes carry inherent delivery risk if deployed too quickly or without sufficient learning. For the New Hospital Programme, the principal implementation risk is therefore not technical feasibility, but unmanaged scale and complexity. A phased implementation approach is the primary control for this risk. [39][44][40]

The initial phase should focus on establishing strong foundations: embedding GS1-based supplier requirements, designing the NHP equipment database around GTIN, GIAI, and GLN, and putting in place core integration patterns, governance structures, and validation controls. This phase prioritises correctness and assurance over breadth of deployment. [18][29]

Subsequent phases should introduce GS1-enabled workflows at early NHP sites, focusing on commissioning, asset registration, and maintenance integration with local CMMS/EAM systems. These sites act as controlled learning environments, allowing workflows, data models, and governance arrangements to be tested and refined before wider rollout. [20][40]

Only once these foundations are proven should the programme scale to additional NHP sites and, where appropriate, integration with legacy hospitals and national platforms such as NETIS. This staged approach reduces delivery risk, supports continuous improvement, and ensures that national rollout is based on demonstrated capability rather than assumption. [41][40]

11.2 Supplier Maturity and Barcode Readiness

Variation in supplier readiness represents a significant implementation risk for GS1-based programmes. While many large manufacturers are already GS1-mature and UDI-compliant, smaller or specialist suppliers may lack experience in allocating identifiers, producing conformant barcodes, or maintaining high-quality product master data. If unmanaged, this variation can undermine data quality and operational confidence. [7][18]

This risk is best addressed through a combination of clear requirements, structured onboarding, and proportionate support. NHS Supply Chain and NHP procurement frameworks provide a natural control point by mandating GS1 membership, correct GTIN allocation across packaging levels, and compliance with barcode quality standards as conditions of participation. [18][19]

Supporting measures including guidance, tooling, and access to GS1 UK support services[CS9] , can accelerate supplier maturity without lowering standards. Where necessary, compliance can be reinforced through procurement levers, phased enforcement, and performance monitoring, ensuring that non-conformant data does not propagate into national systems. [7][18]

By treating supplier maturity as a managed risk rather than an assumption, the programme can maintain data integrity while avoiding unnecessary exclusion of smaller suppliers that are critical to clinical services.

11.3 Change management, training and compliance

Even where technical standards and supplier readiness are in place, implementation can fail if scanning and data capture are not consistently adopted by front-line staff. The primary behavioural risk is therefore partial or inconsistent compliance, leading to gaps in data and erosion of trust in the system. [10]

Mitigating this risk requires visible leadership sponsorship and clear articulation of purpose. Clinical, estates, and digital leaders must consistently frame GS1-enabled workflows as safety and efficiency enablers rather than administrative burdens. This is particularly important in high-pressure clinical and engineering environments. [10]

Training should be role-specific and practical, focusing on how scanning and identification support day-to-day work rather than on abstract standards. Local champions and early adopters play a key role in reinforcing good practice, while ongoing monitoring of scanning rates and data quality provides objective feedback on adoption. Where compliance is low, targeted support and workflow refinement should be prioritised over punitive measures. [42][10]

By embedding GS1 standards into routine practice through leadership, training, and feedback, the programme reduces reliance on individual behaviour and increases the likelihood of sustained, system-wide adoption.

11.4 Key risks and mitigations

The principal risks associated with adopting GS1 standards within the NHP relate to data quality, system integration, and organisational change rather than to the standards themselves. [29][18]

Poor data quality represents a significant risk if identifiers are incorrectly allocated, incomplete, or inconsistently applied. This risk can be mitigated through clear supplier requirements, automated validation of GS1 identifiers, barcode verification processes, and active data stewardship supported by monitoring and remediation workflows. Supplier performance scorecards and onboarding controls further reinforce compliance. [7][18][19]

Integration risk arises where local systems are unable to exchange data reliably with national platforms. This is mitigated by adherence to open standards such as FHIR, EPCIS, and OpenAPI, use of reference architectures, and phased testing that allows issues to be identified and resolved before large-scale rollout. Designing the national database as a harmonisation layer rather than a replacement for local systems further reduces disruption. [29][40][22]

Organisational and behavioural risks include change fatigue, inconsistent scanning adoption, and variable engagement across clinical, estates, and procurement teams. These risks can be mitigated through visible leadership sponsorship, co-design of workflows with front-line staff, role-specific training, and ongoing performance monitoring. Alignment with existing national initiatives such as Scan4Safety and NETIS helps reinforce expectations and sustain momentum. [10][41]

12. Conclusion

GS1 standards offer a mature, widely adopted foundation for equipment identification, traceability and interoperability in healthcare, with a strong evidence base from Scan4Safety and international UDI implementations. For the New Hospital Programme, building the national equipment database on GS1 identifiers (GTIN, GIAI, GLN), EPCIS event data and Digital Link creates a robust platform that: [2][5][13][10]

·         Improves patient safety through precise traceability, rapid recalls, and reduced errors. [10]

·         Enhances efficiency and value via better inventory control, reduced unwarranted variation, and evidence-based capital planning. [37][10]

·         Supports regulatory compliance and assurance aligned with UDI and emerging UK MDR requirements. [14][13][18]

·         Enables modern digital capabilities, including interoperable APIs, GS1 Digital Link, and digital twinning of critical assets. [20]

By embedding GS1 standards and associated governance into the NHP equipment database from the outset and aligning closely with NETIS and NHS Supply Chain, NHS England can ensure that new hospitals are not only physically modern but also underpinned by high-quality, interoperable equipment data that supports safe, efficient, and resilient care for decades to come. [53][39][40][41]

 

Glossary

Term

What it means

What it is / how it is used / why it matters

GS1 – One Global Standards One

A global organisation that sets and maintains standards for identifying, capturing and sharing information.

GS1 is a not-for-profit standards body whose identifiers and barcodes are used worldwide, including across the NHS. For the New Hospital Programme (NHP), GS1 provides a common, internationally recognised way to identify equipment consistently across suppliers, Trusts, and national systems.

GTIN – Global Trade Item Number

A unique number that identifies a specific product.

GTIN identifies the product model or configuration and is used globally. In healthcare it commonly acts as the Device Identifier (UDI-DI). Within NHP, GTINs serve as the canonical product identifier, enabling consistent procurement, benchmarking, and regulatory compliance.

GIAI – Global Individual Asset Identifier

A unique identifier for a single physical asset.

A GIAI distinguishes one individual piece of equipment from all others of the same model. It enables lifecycle tracking of capital equipment, including commissioning, maintenance, movement, and disposal across the NHP estate.

GLN – Global Location Number

A unique identifier for a location or organisation.

GLNs identify sites, buildings, wards, rooms, or legal entities. They provide precise location context for assets and events, supporting estates integration, traceability, and accurate recording of where equipment is installed and used.

UDI – Unique Device Identification

A regulatory system for uniquely identifying medical devices.

UDI requires devices to carry a standardised identifier consisting of a device identifier and production identifiers. GS1 is an accredited issuing agency, with GTIN commonly used as the UDI-DI. UDI supports recalls, vigilance, and patient safety.

GS1 DataMatrix

A two-dimensional barcode commonly used on medical devices.

GS1 DataMatrix can encode a GTIN along with batch/lot number, serial number, and expiry date. It is the preferred barcode for UDI compliance and enables accurate scanning in clinical environments with limited label space.

GS1 Digital Link

A way of expressing GS1 identifiers as web links.

GS1 Digital Link allows a scanned barcode to resolve to online resources such as instructions for use, safety alerts, or maintenance records. For NHP, it provides a flexible mechanism to connect physical equipment to trusted digital information.

Scan4Safety

An NHS programme that uses barcode scanning to improve safety and efficiency.

Scan4Safety is based on scanning person, product, and place using GS1 standards. It has demonstrated reductions in errors, improved recall performance, and significant time and cost savings, providing evidence for national adoption.

Person–Product–Place

A model linking who received what and where.

This model underpins Scan4Safety workflows by linking patient identity, the product or device used, and the location of care. It enables accurate documentation and traceability while allowing patient data to remain within local clinical systems.

EPCIS – Electronic Product Code Information Services

A standard for recording and sharing event data.

EPCIS captures what happened, where, when, and why using GS1 identifiers. In NHP, EPCIS supports lifecycle traceability of equipment, including commissioning, movement, maintenance, and disposal, across multiple systems.

Canonical Identifier

The single authoritative identifier for an item.

Canonical identifiers reconcile multiple local or legacy codes referring to the same item. In NHP, GTINs and GIAIs act as canonical identifiers, enabling harmonisation without replacing local asset tags or catalogue codes.

Master Data

Core reference data used across systems.

Master data includes product definitions, identifiers, and classifications. High-quality master data is essential for interoperability and analytics. In NHP, product master data is primarily supplier-owned and curated centrally.

Data Governance

The framework for managing data quality and ownership.

Data governance defines who owns data, how it is maintained, and how quality issues are resolved. Strong governance ensures that NHP equipment data remains accurate, trusted, and scalable over time.

Digital Twin

A digital representation of a physical asset.

A digital twin mirrors the state and performance of real equipment using data feeds. GS1 identifiers provide the foundation for equipment digital twins within NHP, enabling predictive maintenance and lifecycle analysis.

CMMS / EAM – Computerised Maintenance Management System / Enterprise Asset Management

Systems for managing maintenance and assets.

These systems manage local asset records and maintenance workflows within Trusts. NHP integrates with CMMS/EAM using GS1 identifiers rather than replacing existing systems.

NETIS – NHS Equipment and Inventory Tracking System

A national NHS equipment and inventory platform.

NETIS provides national visibility of equipment and inventory data. Alignment with NHP ensures consistency between new hospitals and the wider NHS estate.

Unwarranted Variation

Differences in practice not explained by patient need.

In equipment management, unwarranted variation refers to inconsistent device choices or utilisation. Standardised identifiers and national data enable benchmarking and evidence-based standardisation.

API – Application Programming Interface

A way for computer systems to communicate with each other.

An API defines how software systems exchange data in a controlled and standardised way. In the NHP context, APIs enable interoperability between national platforms, supplier systems, CMMS/EAM systems, and clinical or estates applications without tight coupling.

FHIR – Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources

A standard for exchanging healthcare information digitally.

FHIR is an HL7 standard widely adopted within the NHS for interoperable digital health systems. In the NHP equipment architecture, FHIR can be used to represent devices and assets consistently and to exchange equipment-related data between clinical, operational, and national systems.

RBAC – Role-Based Access Control

A method of restricting system access based on user roles.

RBAC ensures that users can only view or modify data appropriate to their role (for example Trust-level versus national access). In the NHP equipment database, RBAC supports information governance, security, and audit requirements by limiting access to sensitive operational data.

UDI-DI – Unique Device Identifier (Device Identifier)

The fixed part of a medical device identifier.

The UDI-DI identifies the manufacturer and model or version of a medical device. In GS1-based implementations, the GTIN commonly fulfils this role. UDI-DI supports regulatory compliance, recalls, and consistent identification of device types.

UDI-PI – Unique Device Identifier (Production Identifier)

The variable part of a medical device identifier.

The UDI-PI captures production-specific data such as batch or lot number, serial number, and expiry date. Together with the UDI-DI, it enables precise traceability, recall targeting, and post-market surveillance of medical devices.

Canonical Product Master

A single national reference for product definitions.

A canonical product master reconciles multiple supplier and local catalogues into one authoritative dataset, supporting procurement aggregation, analytics, and consistent reporting across NHP.
